Integration Report

The Integration Report aggregates the knowledge derived in mitigation reports into a coherent storyline depicting a smart water network.

Sources/Treatment

Despite the notion that strategic planning to secure a city's water production is vital to meeting water demands, Cape Town responded late to an impending water crisis that could have easily been stymied by accurate water-security forecasts. Governments need time to conduct appropriate assessment and strategy development for long-term sustainability of new desalination plants [22]. Ergo, proper implementation of current mitigation strategies — such as alternative water production methods, including desalination plants — would require years of planning and construction. Additionally, the water distribution network has to be expanded and upgraded to match the increase in water flow.

An accurate water consumption forecasting system would allow the quantification of water consumption and incoming wastewater, which could then be redirected to seawater desalination and wastewater treatment plants to create a sustainable and efficient water cycle. Furthermore, sufficient amount of time and information for decision bodies to implement availability of alternative water sources and the information necessary to optimize the water cycle would be provided by a water-security forecasting system based on seasonality and long-term meteorological forecasts. Complex, yet sustainable water sources would be long-term, large scale, seawater desalination based on reverse osmosis or water recycling plants, that filter biomass sludge, to guarantee efficient water usage and provide biofuel for both itself and potentially other energy-intensive processes.

Distribution

Accurate forecasting technologies, integrated in a smart water network, would be able to address challenges in water distribution. Forecasting requires seasonal data, such as rainfall quantities and water consumption numbers. In precision agriculture and urban water usage, water tap metering is used to collect this consumption data. After data collection, this data is communicated with stakeholders in charge of forecasting and water distribution strategies to optimize the functional capacity of the integrated water network.

In anticipation of a water crisis, mitigation strategies on long-term water redistribution can contribute to prevent a Day Zero. A potential water shortage can be predicted based on projected income of natural water, projected loss (e.g., due to evaporation, invasive species, and leakages), and expected consumption. If this data is known, these potential shortages can be mitigated. Effective collaboration and communication among relevant stakeholders, i.e. households, companies, agriculture, desalination plants, dam management, etc., remains crucial. Redistribution could be affected by importing water, recycling water, desalinating water, or applying regional water restrictions in times of crisis.

The other critical problem that could be solved by optimising the distribution system in the lack of recycling or downcycling of gray water. A distribution system that does not mix black and grey water, but transports the grey water to places where it can be reused immediately, such as gardens, farms or toilets should be built and implemented.

Usage

Along with mitigation strategies, such as urban water metering [23] and precision agriculture [24], water usage can be reduced through the use of a smart water network. Usage data could be used to influence water restriction levels to reduce usage over a period of time. Dam levels must be monitored continuously to ensure consistency of water consumption levels in both agricultural and urban contexts. On the one hand, monitoring should ensure enough water is available for the future. On the other hand, consumers should also pay attention to their water usage, and will only do that if citizens realize water is scarce. To achieve this, accurate communication of the status quo is crucial to ensuring consumers are willing to reduce their water consumption sufficiently [25][26]. Data about dam levels and predictions about water availability should be integrated with proposed technologies in the urban and agricultural sector.

Wastewater

When it comes to processing wastewater, it is important for administrators to know the quantity of water to be processed, so as not to overload the system. Urban water consumption data can be used to bolster data libraries and predictive capabilities of water forecasting systems to get an accurate prediction of water quantity. Important data on wastewater characteristics can be given by predictive models of water consumption. Furthermore, analyzing spatio-temporal data on wastewater generation can be used by wastewater treatment facilities not only to manage treatment load in real-time, but also predictively manage peak load times and ensure maximum treatment efficiency.

Additional levels of wastewater treatment would decrease contamination levels of water expulsion near marine outfalls [27], providing new intake water for the desalination process. Because of this, the quality drinking water will be further secured in order to reduce the risk of health threats to consumers. Furthermore, to reduce the energy consumption of the whole process, treated water can be directly transferred to desalination plants, providing a potentially less contaminated water source.
